Colour · palette

Tonal ramps

Every brand colour is a 100–900 scale, generated in OKLCH so the steps are perceptually even, with the canonical brand hex pinned to its natural step (marked •). Across every ramp, 100–300 are tints for backgrounds and text on dark, and 700 is the AA-safe text colour on white (marked AA). Click any step to copy its hex.

Colour · neutrals

Greys for light and dark

A purple-tinted neutral ramp for light surfaces, and a separate ramp tuned for dark surfaces (borders and muted text sitting on purple). Primary Grey is pinned at grey-700.

Typography

Montserrat, in weight contrast

The brand mandates Gotham offline and Montserrat online. This is a web product, so Montserrat is the system typeface: a single geometric sans carrying the whole system through weight and scale contrast. Display tightens to -0.02em; body stays at 0.

Four weights, 400 to 700. Regular (400) is the floor: Light (300) is not loaded and never used, Montserrat Light reads too thin at screen sizes and hurts legibility. 700 is the ceiling, 800/900 are not loaded, headlines never go heavier than Bold. Emphasis comes from scale and weight contrast, never from a lighter weight.

Headline cadence (brand voice)

Connect Ideas: Transform Transport

Two-register: the line sets light (400) and only the key phrase carries bold (700), the same weight contrast on every section title. Title case, used sparingly for hero and major statements. Solid white type; emphasis comes from the weight jump and scale, never from yellow.

Spacing & layout

Rhythm, not a uniform gap

A 10-step rem scale. Vary section rhythm (generous separations, tight groupings); don't use one gap everywhere. Content caps at 1180px (--maxw) with generous margins; dark hero and image bands break out to full viewport width. The column grid every page sits on is documented below.

Grid rules: Grid for 2D (package comparison, image bands), Flexbox for 1D rows. Responsive card rows use repeat(auto-fit, minmax(280px, 1fr)). Cards only where they're the right affordance: no nested cards, no endless identical icon+heading+text grids.
The grid

Twelve columns, and phones come first

Every page sits on a set of invisible equal columns, and every block (hero, cards, text, photos) spans a whole number of them, so everything lines up. Desktop gets 12 columns, tablet 8, phone 4.

Mobile first, by order. The phone layout is designed first; desktop is the upgrade, never the other way round. Hard rules: no sideways scrolling, ever; anything tappable is at least 44px tall; body text never drops below 1rem; the page's main button sits within thumb reach; every page is checked at 375px wide before it ships.

Spacing · page architecture

The block wall

Pages are not edge-to-edge bands: each section is a floating block, a translucent surface (85% opacity, --blk-a) with --blk-r 12px corners and 26px cracks between blocks, so the page backdrop glints through. Four block surfaces: dark, deep, light and tint. The final sections of a page (newsletter, organiser strip, footer) opt out and form a solid full-bleed foot: 100% opacity, square corners, no cracks. (This documentation site itself uses a plain canvas; the block wall is the rule for the congress pages.)

Iconography

One icon set: Font Awesome Pro, Light

Every icon on the site comes from Font Awesome Pro in its Light style, one consistent line weight across the whole product. It is loaded via a Font Awesome Kit (<script src="https://kit.fontawesome.com/c81e889324.js" crossorigin="anonymous"></script>); ERTICO supplies the Pro Kit. Icons are drawn as text, so they inherit their colour from the surrounding text and are sized with font-size, never fixed width/height.

Usage: a standard icon is <i class="fa-light fa-NAME" aria-hidden="true"></i>; brand and social marks use the brands style, <i class="fa-brands fa-NAME"></i>. Decorative icons carry aria-hidden="true"; an icon-only control (a button with no text) gets an aria-label so it is still announced.

Imagery & media

How photos, video and textures are treated

The signature is the Purple Overlay: brand purple laid over imagery with mix-blend-mode: multiply so a photo reads as unmistakably ours and text stays legible. Below are the treatments the site uses, worked in real markup, then the three composed photographic routes, then video.

Photo + Purple Overlay

Per the toolkit: a solid Primary or Secondary purple laid over the image with mix-blend-mode: multiply at 80% opacity, covering the full, two-thirds, half or one-third of the image height from the foot. The height is chosen so the photo stays visible and the copy stays legible.

Logo wall

Third-party marks always sit on a white plate, so partners keep their official colourways on any surface. Three variants: the partner strip (organiser and host lockups, always full colour), the exhibitor wall (many marks, full colour: the industry norm, and every exhibitor sees their own brand instantly; the shared white plate keeps the wall uniform), and the big wall (15+ marks, e.g. Development Advisors). All display assets come from the normalised logo sets.

Big wall rule: the breathing grid with equal visual weight. At most five marks per row (tiles minmax 160px), wide gaps (36px row, 44px column), no boxes, no motion. Every logo is scaled to cover the same visual area, not the same height: height = √(targetArea / aspect) with target 4600px², height clamped 26–60px, width capped 170px (a small script does this per logo at load). A wide wordmark gets less height, a square crest gets more, and mixed proportions stop reading as a mess. Reference build: the Development Advisors wall on the homepage.

Signature component

The host-cities globe

A dark, self-spinning 3D globe that plots every ITS World Congress host city, with pill labels that reveal as each city rotates to the front and Birmingham singled out as the destination. Drag to spin; let go and it keeps turning. Built on cobe (self-hosted), the dots and labels are HTML overlays positioned from each city's real latitude/longitude every frame. It is created lazily when scrolled into view and torn down when it leaves, so it costs nothing off-screen.

    Plots all 33 host cities from Paris 1994 to Birmingham 2027. This is the same component inlined into the congress homepage, one source of truth.

    Logos & lockups

    The official marks, on light and dark

    Real SVGs from the toolkit. Logos are a large part of what the site displays, so the rules matter: on purple → white / reversed; on light → colour. Clear space and a minimum size are respected on every placement (ITS Birmingham 2027 = 1×'E' minimum).

    On purple → white. Reversed mark on any dark or purple surface.
    On light → colour. Full-colour mark on white or light tints.
    On photo → white over overlay. Never place a logo directly on a busy photo.
    Lockups · which one, when

    Choosing the right lockup

    Five lockups, each supplied in colour and white. Three questions decide it: the space (wide → landscape; tall or square → portrait; under ~24px → brandmark), the date (keep it on first-impression surfaces; drop it where the date is already on the page or the placement outlives the edition), and the surface (light → colour; purple or photo → white, per the rule above).

    Brandmark Icon

    Icon duty and the minimum-size fallback: favicon and app icons, tiny avatars, watermarks, motifs. Use it anywhere the wordmark would drop below its ~24px minimum.

    Export quirk, and why the normalised set exists: the official SVG exports are inconsistently cropped (the colour landscape files carry roughly half their canvas as empty clear space; the white ones are tight). The design system displays tight-cropped copies from assets/logos/its-birmingham-2027/normalised/ so colour and white always render at the same optical size. Use the normalised set for layout; keep the originals for print and official handoff.

    Clear space

    Keep a minimum margin of 1×'E' of the wordmark clear on all sides. Nothing intrudes.

    Minimum size

    Below the minimum, the wordmark stops being legible. Use the brandmark-only lockup for tight spaces.
